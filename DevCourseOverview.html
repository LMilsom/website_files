Introduction to the Economics of Development

D0E32A

Luke Heath Milsom, KU Leuven, 2025[1] 

Hi! Welcome to the introduction to the economics of development course at KUL. This is a third-year course, and as such, we will immediately be reading and critiquing research at the frontier. This is not an easy course; we will be ambitious in the depth and breadth of material covered.

I expect students to come to lectures having done the preparatory reading and to participate in individual study each week. Lectures will be incomprehensible to those who have not prepared sufficiently for them.

Lectures will be recorded and uploaded on Friday evening each week — technical issues occur regularly, so I cannot guarantee that each recording will be available. You cannot take this course if you expect to regularly miss lectures for any reason.

This course is assessed in three ways. First, students are continuously assessed in 8 weekly quizzes. These quizzes are short and relatively straightforward. I ignore the worst quiz grade for each student, and the average of the remaining quizzes counts as 10% of your final grade. Second, the last three weeks of this course are a group project, which counts as 30% of your final grade. Third, the final 60% of your grade comes from a closed-book exam in the normal exam period. This will not be a multiple-choice exam.

The course content is in three parts.

Part 1 aims to bring all students up to speed on foundational econometrics and statistics — students come from a wide variety of disciplines to this course, and this optional part aims to bring everyone to roughly the same level on some necessary concepts.

Part 2 is the main body of the course and consists of 8 weeks of lectures. Each week, we will tackle a different question, core to the economics of development. Lectures will be interactive, and it is highly recommended to attend in person.

Part 3 is the group project. Students will form groups of 3 to 5 (themselves) and write a project of approximately 2,000 words. Groups come up with their own question based on one or more topics discussed in the lectures. I will meet with each group individually to discuss their project.

Part 1: Econometrics Primer

This optional, online, part of the course is designed to get all students up to speed on the foundational econometrics and statistics necessary to think and argue as an economist, and understand and critique the cutting-edge research we will discuss. It comprises of a series of small videos that students are encouraged to watch as and when they want. The material in this part of the course is only directly assessed through a short quiz at the end of week 2; however, a solid understanding will be necessary to perform well in the exam and fully comprehend the lectures.

Part 2: Lectures

Here, I detail the main concepts and readings for each week/ question. Students are expected to read the introduction to each of the main papers before the lecture (but are not expected to understand it all!). It is advised that students also read the additional reading, but this is not mandatory. This course does not have a textbook, but students are encouraged to consult the texts under general resources for background reading.

W4 Why do people stay poor?

  • Main paper(s): Balboni et al., (2022).
  • Theory: Poverty traps, and misallocation.
  • Econometrics: Identification, endogeneity, RCTs.
  • Additional reading: Barrett and Swallow (2006), Kraay and McKenzie (2014), Turnbull et al. (2024).
  • Week 4 slides.

W5 Are natural resources a solution or a trap?

  • Main paper(s): Sachs and Warner (2001), Jensen and Watchekon (2004), Gollin et al. (2016).
  • Theory: Political economy of development, Dutch disease.
  • Econometrics: Selection on observables, fixed effects
  • Additional reading: van der Ploeg (2011), Corden and Neary (1982).
  • Week 5 slides.

W6 Can we manufacture prosperity?

  • Main paper(s): Rodrik (2016).
  • Theory: Structural transformation, International trade.
  • Econometrics: Fixed effects, time series
  • Additional reading: Herrendorf, Rogerson, and Valentinyi (2014), Fan et al. (2023)
  • Week 6 slides.

W7 Are institutions key?

  • Main paper(s): Acemoglu et al. (2001), Glaeser et al. (2004), Albouy (2012).
  • Theory: Institutions.
  • Econometrics: Instrumental variables.
  • Additional reading: Why Nations Fail (book) Acemoglu and Robinson.
  • Guest lecture on 7.11 from Dr von Burgsdorff, an economist at the UN with many years of experience working in the aid sector.
  • Week 7 slides.

W8 Are credit constraints constraining development?

  • Main paper(s): Duflo et al. (2013), Egger et al. (2023).
  • Theory: General Equilibrium effects.
  • Econometrics: RCT + IV.
  • Additional reading: Meager (2019), Banerjee et al. (2024).
  • Week 8 slides.

W9 The City: Humanities' greatest invention?

  • Main paper(s): Bryan et al. (2020), Bryan et al. (2014).
  • Theory: Urban and Spatial Economics.
  • Econometrics: LATE and ITT.
  • Additional reading: Triumph of the City (Book, Glaeser), Harari (2020), Tsivanidis (2023).
  • Week 9 slides.

W10 How will climate change interact with the development challenge?

  • Main paper(s): Jedwab et al. (2023), Gollin and Wolfersberger (2024).
  • Theory: Quantitative spatial economics.
  • Econometrics: Difference in differences.
  • Week 10 slides.
